# LA's Famous Traffic Traps Harmful Air Pollutants in the Air Overnight

## The City's Qualms

Los Angeles is commonly associated with two things: traffic and smog. LA residents spend more time sitting in their cars than almost any other city in the world, and the city's air quality has long been a public health concern. What is less understood is exactly how, and when, the pollutants from all those cars actually shows up in the air we breathe.

## The Problem

NO₂, also known as nitrogen dioxide, is a pollutant produced directly by vehicle combustion engines and a key marker of smog. While it is well established that traffic contributes to NO₂ pollution, the precise relationship between traffic patterns and measurable air quality changes is less understood. Los Angeles presents an ideal setting to examine this question — the city's sprawling, car-dependent infrastructure means millions of vehicle trips are made daily across an extensive freeway network, What can the city's traffic patterns tell us about the air's NO₂ levels?

## The Solution

This project used a predictive model to test whether the previous day's traffic activity on LA freeways could predict nighttime NO₂ levels at nearby air quality monitoring stations. By pairing hourly traffic sensor data from Caltrans with NO₂ measurements from the EPA, and focusing specifically on overnight air quality readings, the model was able to explain roughly 45% of the variation in nighttime NO₂ levels using traffic volume, speed, and congestion data alone. The findings suggest that heavier traffic days are associated with worse air quality that night.
